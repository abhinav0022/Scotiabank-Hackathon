Introduction

As technology rapidly develops around us, becoming further ingrained into our everyday lives, we find it inevitable that banks will adapt, and take advantage of these changes. Here, we will explore three possibilities for that future.

With mobile banking , we expect an increased demand for convenience .
With quantum computing and other technologies, we will see opportunities and threats to security.
With machine learning , we will see better models for risk management.

The Mobile User

It’s no secret that consumers are wanting to access their data from anywhere. With the development of smartphones, being constantly connected has become a part of life.

This holds true for banking. 89% of people use mobile banking in some way, and it’s not hard to see why [1]. Mobile banking makes this data access easy - from the managing of accounts, to bill payments - all over a mobile app.

The Rise of Mobile Banking

With mobile banking growing at 5x the rate of online banking, we should only expect its prevalence to continue [1]. One obvious development then, is the greater demand for mobile functionality. From arranging mortgages to making investments, consumers will want more features that traditionally required in person meetings. Of course, with this, we will require more security, as well as calculating risk accurately, and rapidly.

These developments will need to be backed by technological breakthroughs however. Some examples could be with virtual banking assistants, or augmented reality.

Virtual Assistants

Virtual assistants could take on much of the work of a human bank teller. They could possibly advise on financial management, investments, and even arrange loans and mortgages, all by conversing with a program on your phone. Although current virtual assistants like siri leave much to be desired, it’s almost certain that in the next 10 years, we will have programs that can outperform human bank tellers in the majority of standardized tasks.

Augmented Reality

Augmented reality is an emerging, but promising technology - adding virtual objects to real time video to ‘augment’ the reality you’re seeing. With mobile banking, it can be used for things like giving you an expected interest rate when pointing your camera at a car in the dealership, or a mortgage rate when pointed at a house.

Digital Security

Security is at the forefront of all banking services. We currently have measures in place to help ensure consumers keep their accounts secure, and encrypted protection for the billions of dollars being transferred between financial institutions each day [2]. But as technology progresses, it brings with it both new threats, and opportunities, that we will have to face.

Here, we will discuss two sides to that future: increasing consumer security through biometric, machine learning, and mobile verification, as well as the threats to encryption looming on the horizon.

Consumer Security

Biometrics

The increased demand for convenience with mobile banking lends itself to a number of new security opportunities.

One is the inclusion of biometrics. While current security methods use authentication such as pin numbers and government ID, a much more secure method of authentication would be through unique biometrics - such as fingerprints. With nearly all phones, and some laptops, sporting fingerprint scanners, it may not be such a stretch to suggest similar means of security on card readers or ATMs.

Fradulent Spending

Another may be the use of machine learning to track fraudulent spending. Well trained neural nets can be very effective at spotting patterns and conversely, suspicious breaks in those patterns. By feeding in typical spending activity, location data, and any other information you may have on your consumer, such a model could be able to detect fraudulent transactions to a higher degree of accuracy than our current automated systems [3].

Mobile Security

Finally, the increase in mobile banking means that your customer could be reachable wherever they are, and allows them to take appropriate steps to rapidly respond to any breaches in security. With a more robust mobile platform, and possibly the help of a virtual assistant, they could lock accounts, freeze payments, or even dispute charges without needing to speak to a human representative.

The Encryption Dilemma

Encryption

In terms of back end security, encryption is the key that ties everything together. All modern financial transfers are encrypted, typically with AES protocol. This protocol encodes text using a secret key - making it gibberish without using said key to decode it. The longer your key, the more secure your encryption. With current computing, cracking 128 bit AES encryption would take hundreds of trillions of years. But, quantum computers can change all of this .

Quantum Computing

Quantum computers rely on quantum bits (or qubits) rather than traditional binary bits. Qubits take advantage of two phenomena: superposition - giving them the ability to represent a 1 and 0 simultaneously, and entanglement - meaning each qubit is able to affect and work with other qubits around it. These properties make quantum computers better at some tasks than traditional computers, such as finding prime factors of very large numbers, which so happens to be what all our encryption is based on [4].

Closer Than We Think

Although seemingly far in the future, the possibility of quantum computers breaking encryption is very real, with a 53 qubit computer already developed by google [5]. Although breaking AES 128 would require millions of such qubits, experts predict that this previously impossible task could be accomplished by a quantum computer in the next couple of decades [6]. And this doesn’t even include less secure but still very popular encryption methods such as RSA.

How Can We Protect Aginst This?

Where do we go from here? According to current predictions, AES encryption could still be secure with 256 bit keys. However, AES encryption also requires a different encryption method to distribute its secret keys, which is often done with quantum-vulnerable RSA encryption. One solution for this could be through quantum key distribution (QKD), which uses photons. The advantage of QKD is that an eavesdropper would destroy the incoming data, and due to quantum uncertainty, be unable to accurately recreate it - easily allowing the receiver to detect tampering [7].

Risk Management

A large part of a modern bank’s work is risk management. Determining appropriate interest rates for loans, finding good investments to grow capital, are all traditionally done through a mix of advanced algorithms, equations, and human judgement. In this vein, the future provides a promising new technology to augment decision making - machine learning.

Machine Learning

Machine learning tools are fantastic at picking up on patterns that humans may miss. Unsupervised models especially, can be especially effective at pattern finding, but like all models, require huge volumes of data to train properly. Banking in particular is well suited for this - with millions of daily customers, performing hundreds of millions of transactions, data volume could very well be a nonissue [8].

Examples

Machine learning can have multiple applications. Credit score, transaction history, and any number of other factors can be fed into a trained model to determine someone’s risk of defaulting on a loan. Market analytical data, company performance, and historical information may be used to predict investment performance. The key advantage is that machine learning models can pour over more data and find connections that humans or traditional algorithms simply can’t.
